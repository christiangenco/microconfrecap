Onboarding touches a little on copywriting, user research, and customer success.

People don't want software, they want their problems solved.

This mountain is scary and hard because of a lot of hidden work. The “Hidden Work” in your SaaS trial includes things like….

  • Forcing your users to figure out what to do first
  • Forcing your users to watch a 22 minute video before they can even get started
  • Forcing your users to become an expert on your product before they even know if they like it

Product-Centric Work

Product-Centric Work (aka, the work of figuring out how to use a new product)

Here are 3 examples of emails you may have gotten in your inbox.

1. The Vacation Photos Approach

Your user just wants to do something simple - like importing their data. Don't make an onboarding experience. That makes it a lot harder to get started. Don't make your users watch your vacation videos before they can eat the deviled eggs.

2. The “Too Much Too Soon” Approach

Don't give people too many options. It doesn't increase the likelihood they'll take action on it.

3. The “Bad Intern” Approach

Don't make your users figure out what they need.

People are signing up because they have a problem they want to solve. If you make it hard, only the most committed will get through your bad onboarding.

Internal Work

Internal Work: aka, the work of forcing yourself to do the thing you think you might want to do

What’s the “job” that your customer hires you to do?

  • What’s going on in their world that made someone consider your product?
  • What outcome are they looking for?
  • What do they believe about their problem?

This is effectively the Jobs to be Done framework.

Your customer’s biggest problem is unlikely to be that they want to try your features.

Your customer may hate software, but still have the pain your software solves.

Asking your cusomters "hey, what's going on in your world?" is a gold mine of data.

When you're doing this customer research, look for outcome phrases:

  • “so that”
  • “because”
  • “I want to”
  • “that way”
  • “so I can”
  • “I’ll be able to”
  • “what I want to do is”

Where do you put this research? Try:

  • Airstory: free tool for clipping, organizing, and writing with voice of customer data
  • Nomnom: aggregate customer feedback on an existing project
  • Usability-Hub: get feedback on usability

Don't just pretend to go through UX research ("UX theatre"). Ex: find a random quote and try to pass it off as a customer quote.

How do you write emails?

What should an onboarding email do? Get your customers to come back. Everyone is always leaving.

How? Three things:

  1. Message-Matching: Get them to overcome their internal hurdles.
  2. Instructions: eliminate the product-centric work involved with doing what you're asking them to do
  3. Call to action: invite them to complete the action they need to take now.

BananaBlaster fictional example:

Don't make people watch a 35 minute video, just tell them how to solve their problems.

You should also pay attention to how your customer is going to be using this information. Are they going to be on the subway and can't load your links?

You can say "there are 35 ways to use this project" but it doesn't work as well as joining the conversation happening in your reader's head and solving the problem they're having right now.

Making it easy to get started works. Harvest's "Create a Project" email hasn't changed in years.

Takeaway

To help your customers get started, get the right mindset:

  • You’re teaching people something new
  • You’re solving their problems
  • You’re eliminating hidden work

Questions

Do you start off users with the first email in each action?

Yes, then drill down.

In your examples of emails, they're all plaintext. We've noticed those do a lot better (because people are sick of the Mailchimp template). Our current onboarding series can't be plaintext because they have screenshots.

Yeah, start with plaintext. Also test what works best for you.

You touched on the "what's up in your world" research emails and the "here's our product" call to action emails. Are those separate? In the same email?

Do it separately. Keep people tightly focused on a single action.

How do you consider in-app onboarding?

You want both in-app and email onboarding. Email onboarding is for when they leave.

Also, a lot of onboarding in-app happens right when you sign up, but often people sign up at 2am when they're not ready to actually onboard.
