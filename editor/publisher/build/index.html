Story Time

Justin started a showboard shop with two friends in 2003, which was a huge mistake.

He learned a few things.

Lesson 1: Choosing the right customer is more important than what you sell.

Lesson 2: starting small is almost always better than going big.

If Justin had started small, he would've known the Faucklers are bad customers, but with smaller stakes. He could've gotten out for cheaper when he realized he didn't like it.

For an idea to get BIG, it needs to start small -@sivers

Justin Jackson, Microconf 2018

Lesson 3: the way we start businesses is wrong.

The idea people have behind building businesses is that you:

  1. Have a good idea
  2. Launch it
  3. $$$

But this doesn't work! We we evolved it:

  1. Have a good idea
  2. Validate it
  3. Launch it
  4. $$$

Even product/market fit isn't enough, though. You need more than that.

You need more than product/market fit. You need market/founder fit, product/market fit, and product/founder fit.

Justin Jackson, Microconf 2018

Is your audience a group of people that you want to serve every day for the rest of your life? Is this product a good fit for what you want? Does it match up with your values? Does it get you where you want to go?

Are you at Microconf to find a product idea? Are you trying to choosing a target market? Do you want to validate your product idea? Have you launched, but need more customers? Justin can help.

Here's Justin's step-by-step approach for:

  • Identifying who you are, and what you want
  • Choosing a market
  • Discovering what they want
  • Shipping a (small) initial solution

Section 1: Know thyself

Question 1: What's your motivation for wanting to build a product?

People call Justin for coaching all the time trying to get him to validate their idea. "I wanna do arbitrage for cryptocurrency!" - Justin isn't the target market! But he knows you need to figure out why you want to do this.

"I want to build a product so that I can _ ."

Question 2: What do you value?

What core values do you have?

  • Creativity
  • Impact
  • Integrity
  • Adventure
  • Family
  • Wealth

Specifically, how wealthy would you like to be?

  • Very wealthy ($1 million+)
  • Higher than average ($300k / year)
  • Financially secure ($150k / year)
  • Basic needs ($50k - $100k / year)

Section 2: choose your market

Who do you want to serve? Start with a group of people you want to help. A great business isn't about you, and your dream. It's about helping your customers achieve their dreams.

A great business isn't about you, and your dream. It's about helping your customers achieve their dreams.

Justin Jackson, Microconf 2018

A few ideas for finding your market:

  • How do you currently make a living?
  • What kind of customers do you serve at work? (these people are already paying for your expertise)
  • What communities do you belong to?
  • What do you do better than anyone else?

Factors to consider:

  1. How do they spend money?
  2. How big is the market?
  3. How easy are they to reach?

Also consider how easy your audience is to reach.

Section 3: customer research

What are the big pain points that already exist? What problems are simmering in your target market?

What do they search on Google? What kinds of questions are they asking every day?

Here are some tools to help you out:

  • Productvalidator.com
  • Answerthepublic.com
  • Ahrefs.com

Also, do interviews! Phone, skype, or in-person.

Questions to ask in an interview (for B2B):

  1. What's your focus right now at work? What are you looking to improve?
  2. What's something you'd like to accomplish, but can't? What's holding you back?
  3. What was the last product, service, or tool you bought for work?
  4. What's your least favorite tool you have to use right now?
  5. What kinds of tasks are you currently doing in Excel?
  6. What product do you wish existed (but doesn't yet)?

What trends are you seeing in the community? Right now in the Microconf community, GDPR is a hot topic.

Summarize your research

  • What patterns emerged?
  • What would say are the #1 and #2 things your market is struggling with?
  • What would it mean for them if you solved that struggle? We're solving problems for real human beings!

Section 4: your hypothesis

This group wants ${outcome}, but struggle with ${struggle}. I can help them by removing these obstacles. Now, their life looks like this: ${better_life}.

Podcasting example

Many tech businesses want a branded podcast, but struggle with getting listeners. I can help them by:

  • improving their show art + description
  • getting them more ratings + reviews
  • making their episodes easier to share

Now, their podcast has more downloads, and their business has more brand awareness.

Ask questions like "what was the last product you bought from work?"

Before Jason Cohen built WPEngine, he pitched it to people and asked if they would pay for it, then asked if they'd write him a check for their first month.

Other trends to look for (especially in a client's slack):

  • What is your target market investing in? (ex: ReWork podcast is a huge investment from basecamp)
  • What's getting a lot of publicity right now?
  • What are people struggling with?

Summary of the process

Start with a small free offering. If that works, move to a small paid offering. If that works. build a simple product. If that works, put more features in and charge more.

Transistor.fm case study

Here's how this has worked so far for Transistor.fm.

Justin started with some small free offerings of podcasts:

  • ProductPeople.tv
  • MegaMaker
  • Build Your SaaS

In the process, he noticed some trends. 75% of people who bought Marketing for Devs, were podcast listeners, and made a huge effort to come talk to him because they felt like they'd known him for years ("If you make it to Vernon, BC, I'll buy you coffee").

Justin also noticed a trend of tech businesses launching podcasts: StartUp by Gimlet, CodePen Radio, and others.

Questions

Who's your favorite American podcaster?

Adam Clark, for sure.

If you're already two months in with 6-10 customers, is it worth going back and building the audience?

I think it is - there's so much pain if you don't figure out the personal questions of what you want, value, and how much money you want to make. If it keeps growing and doesn't match your values you'll end up in a lot of pain.

Don't get stuck making gambling machines in India.

Is B2C SaaS always a bad idea?

Ugh. Yes, but there's definitely people that make it work.

SaaS B2C businesses are things people desire: Netflix, Spotify, etc. It's hard to deliver that if you're a bootstrapper.

Prosumers are different - that sometimes works.

Do you consider parameters for failures?

I have much better parameters for success now. Ex: if I can't get 500 people on a mailing list, it's probably not going to work.
